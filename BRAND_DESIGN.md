# Beanfolio — Brand & Design Guide

*For designers creating public pages, marketing campaigns, and brand content.*

---

## 1. What Is Beanfolio

Beanfolio is a specialty coffee companion app for iPhone. It lets coffee lovers scan any coffee bag, build a personal collection, and discover new beans matched to their taste — weekly, from local specialty roasters.

The app is available in English and French.

---

## 2. Vision

> **Help every coffee lover know what they're drinking and find what they'll love next.**

Most people who enjoy specialty coffee can't name what they tasted yesterday. Bags pile up, roasters blur together, and great coffees are forgotten. Beanfolio turns that fleeting experience into a lasting, personal archive — and then uses everything you've collected to point you toward your next favourite cup.

This is not a social network. This is not an advertising platform. Beanfolio is a private, personal tool that grows smarter the more you use it.

---

## 3. Positioning

**Category:** Specialty coffee / personal collection

**One-liner:** Scan any coffee bag. Build your collection. Discover what's next.

**Against the field:**
- Not a general coffee app with café ratings and maps (that's Yelp for coffee)
- Not a barista tool or recipe tracker (that's technique-first)
- Not a subscription box or e-commerce app (Beanfolio is agnostic — it works with any bag from anywhere)

Beanfolio sits at the intersection of **personal archive** and **taste discovery**. Think of it like a wine journal that also recommends bottles — but for specialty coffee, and deeply personal.

---

## 4. Brand Values

| Value | What it means in practice |
|---|---|
| **Authenticity** | Recommendations are matched to your palate, not to paid placements. The app is honest about what it knows and doesn't know. |
| **Craft** | Beanfolio celebrates the work that goes into specialty coffee — origin, process, roast — and treats it seriously without being pretentious. |
| **Discovery** | The best coffee you've had shouldn't be the last great one. Every week brings something new, curated for you. |
| **Personal growth** | Your palate is a living thing. The app tracks how it evolves and acknowledges your journey with milestones. |
| **Accessibility** | Available fully in both English and French. Simple enough for a first coffee bag, deep enough for an obsessive collector. |

## 5. Voice & Tone

**Voice characteristics:**
- Warm and knowledgeable, not academic
- Celebratory of personal taste, not prescriptive ("this is correct coffee")
- Direct — no filler, no hype
- Occasionally playful, especially around milestones and discovery

**Do:**
- "Your palate map grows with every rated bean."
- "New beans matched to your taste, every week."
- "Scan. Collect. Discover."
- "From Coffee Curious to Master Taster."

**Don't:**
- Don't use coffee clichés ("wake up and smell the coffee", "bean there done that")
- Don't use tech jargon (no "AI-powered" in headline positions — let the experience speak)
- Don't be elitist — specialty coffee is for anyone curious, not only experts
- Don't oversell — Beanfolio is a quiet companion, not a loud product

**Bilingual note:** French copy should feel natural and idiomatic, not translated. The French audience is the same persona — curious, passionate, local-first. Avoid anglicisms where a natural French equivalent exists.

---

## 6. The User

**Primary persona:** Someone who has started caring about specialty coffee. They buy from local roasters, they notice the difference between origins, and they sometimes forget what they had last month and wish they'd written it down. They are not necessarily an expert — they're curious and growing.

**Secondary persona:** The enthusiast collector. They have strong opinions, they vote for their favourite roasters, their collection has 30+ beans. They love data, radar charts, and the palate map.

**What they care about:**
- Knowing what's in their cup (origin, process, roast level, tasting notes)
- Supporting local specialty roasters
- Their own taste — not a critic's score
- A clean, beautiful experience that respects the coffee

**What they don't care about:**
- Café reviews and maps
- Mass-market coffee chains
- Generic recommendations based on popularity

## 7. Color Palette

The palette is drawn from the physical materials of coffee — the espresso shot, the crema, the bag, the page of a notebook.

| Name | Hex | Feel | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Espresso** | `#3C2A21` | Deep, grounding | Primary dark — headlines, icon body, heavy UI |
| **Amber** | `#D4860A` | Warm, energetic | Accent — CTAs, highlights, flavor tags, the "B" in the logo |
| **Paper** | `#FDFCF8` | Clean, organic | Primary light background — not pure white, slightly warm |
| **Night** | `#1A1109` | Rich dark | Dark-mode backgrounds, depth |

**Extended palette (derived):**
- Use tints of Espresso for secondary text on dark surfaces
- Use tints of Amber for hover states and subtle highlights
- Avoid cool greys — the palette is warm throughout

**Do not use:**
- Pure black (`#000000`) or pure white (`#FFFFFF`) — always use Night or Paper instead
- Saturated greens, blues, or purples — they conflict with the warm identity

## 8. Logo & Icon

### The Medallion

The Beanfolio logo mark is called **The Medallion**. It is a circular badge — an espresso-brown disc with a cream ring border, containing a latte-art rosette pattern and a stylized **"B"** rendered in foam at the top of the rosette.

The medallion evokes:
- The ritual and craft of espresso preparation
- A collector's coin or badge — something earned, something kept
- The warmth of crema on a well-pulled shot

**Light version:** Medallion on Paper (`#FDFCF8`) background. Soft drop shadow beneath the disc.

**Dark version:** Medallion on Night (`#1A1109`) background. The amber center of the rosette becomes more vivid and luminous against the dark field. No drop shadow.

**Monochrome / tinted version:** Full greyscale. Maximum contrast — the cream ring border and rosette lines are the brightest elements against the dark disc.

### Logotype

The wordmark **Beanfolio** uses the product name in full. When pairing the medallion with the wordmark:
- The medallion always sits to the left of the wordmark
- Maintain clear space equivalent to the medallion's radius on all sides
- Never distort, rotate, or recolor the medallion independently of the wordmark

### Usage rules
- Never place the medallion on a background that removes contrast (no amber on amber, no espresso on dark brown)
- The "B" inside the medallion is part of the illustration — it is not the lettermark in isolation
- Do not use the medallion at sizes below 32×32 pt — at small sizes, use the wordmark alone

## 9. Core Product Screens

Understanding these five screens is essential for creating accurate marketing content. Each has a distinct visual identity and narrative role.

### Scanner
**What it is:** The camera view. The user points their phone at a coffee bag — front and back.

**Visual mood:** Focused, live, slightly dramatic. The camera frame is foregrounded. This is the moment of discovery — a coffee bag transforming into data.

**Key visual elements:** Camera viewfinder, instruction text, a coffee bag in frame.

**Narrative:** "Just point and shoot." The magic happens in seconds. No typing, no searching.

---

### Bean Detail
**What it is:** The full profile of a scanned bean — photo, name, roaster, origin, roast level, tasting notes, and a radar chart visualizing six sensory dimensions (acidity, body, sweetness, aroma, bitterness).

**Visual mood:** Editorial, rich, personal. This is where the coffee lives in the collection.

**Key visual elements:** Full-bleed bean photo, sensory radar chart, star rating, tasting notes in a warm card layout.

**Narrative:** "Everything about this coffee, beautifully organized."

---

### Collection
**What it is:** The user's full archive — a grid of every bean they've scanned.

**Visual mood:** Abundant, varied, satisfying. A full grid feels like an achievement.

**Key visual elements:** Grid of bean cards showing bag photos, roaster names, star ratings. Sort and search controls.

**Narrative:** "Your coffee history, at a glance."

---

### Discover
**What it is:** Weekly recommendations from local specialty roasters, personalized to the user's taste profile.

**Visual mood:** Curated, editorial, fresh. A new set of cards appears each week.

**Key visual elements:** Product cards with coffee photos, roaster names, a short AI-generated reason why this bean fits the user's palate. A "Refreshes weekly" pill label.

**Narrative:** "New beans matched to your taste, every week — not sponsored, not random."

---

### Profile / Palate Map
**What it is:** The user's coffee identity — their palate radar (built from all their rated beans), preferred brew methods, and badge progress.

**Visual mood:** Personal, reflective, earned. This screen rewards consistent use.

**Key visual elements:** Palate radar chart (mirrors the bean radar but aggregated across all rated beans), badge grid showing progression from Coffee Curious to Master Taster.

**Narrative:** "Your taste in coffee, mapped."

## 11. Photography & Illustration Style

### Photography
- **Coffee bags:** Shot clean — bag in focus, background neutral or slightly out of focus. Bags from independent, specialty roasters (not grocery store brands). Artisan label design preferred.
- **Hands and ritual:** Hands holding bags, hands on espresso machines, the pour. Warm, ambient light. Morning or golden-hour tones.
- **Cafés and spaces:** Independent specialty cafés. Wood, concrete, warm tungsten light. Quiet, not crowded.
- **People:** Diverse, curious, unpretentious. Not barista-as-performer — barista-as-craftsperson. Coffee as part of a moment, not a lifestyle statement.

**Avoid:**
- Latte art close-ups without context (overused)
- Corporate coffee imagery (chains, disposable cups)
- Cold, blue-toned product photography
- Stock photo aesthetics

### Illustration / Iconography
- Line-weight icons that match the app's system — clean, geometric
- Illustration style: warm, slightly textured, editorial. Think specialty coffee magazine meets app UI.
- The radar chart is a signature visual — it appears in marketing as a motif representing "depth" and "profile"

## 13. What Beanfolio Is Not

Use this as a guardrail when creating content.

- **Not a social app.** There are no feeds, followers, or public profiles. Beanfolio is private and personal.
- **Not a café directory.** It does not show café locations, opening hours, or customer reviews.
- **Not a recipe or brewing app.** It does not tell you how to make coffee — it tells you about the coffee you're making.
- **Not a subscription.** The discovery feature surfaces local roasters, but Beanfolio does not sell coffee or take commissions.
- **Not a coffee chain app.** The entire identity is built around independent specialty coffee culture.

## 14. Bilingual Content — EN / FR

All public-facing content should be produced in both English and French. The French version should feel native — not a translation.

**French brand voice:** Same warmth and directness as English. The French coffee culture embraces specificity and care ("torréfacteur artisanal", "notes de dégustation", "votre profil gustatif") — use the vocabulary naturally.

**Key terms in French:**

| Scan | Scanner / Scannez |

| Tasting notes | Notes de dégustation |
